Chapter 3 Scientific Writing

Skills developed in this chapter: Personal development

3.1 Overall Structure

Whether you’re writing a simple report for a course, an original research article, a review, or even a meta-analysis, any scientific article should contain at least the following:

  • What did you study? (Introduction)
  • Why did you study it? (Introduction)
  • How did you study it? (Methods)
  • What did you observe? (Results)
  • What do you conclude? (Discussion)

Hourglass model

A simple guideline is to start broad, explain the context, get progressively more specific, and finally end broad again, explaining the biological relevance of your findings (fig. 3.1). Some journals require the methods section to be placed at the end of the article but the style in which each section should be written is the same as shown here.

Figure 3.1: The hourglass model of scientific writing. The beginning and end of an article provide context, while the middle explains the specifics of the particular study.

The methods and results sections are the easiest to write. You literally just write down what you have done, and what you have observed as a result of doing so. The aim here is to be clear and objective. The only difficulty is getting used to the style of language in scientific articles, but after reading just several scientific articles within the field of research you are writing about, you should already get the hang of it.

The hard parts are the introduction and the discussion. In both of these sections, The discussion goes beyond the results, covering not only the conclusion, but also the limitations, suggestions for future research, and what relevance the findings have for the field as a whole.

You may have noticed that the abstract is nowhere to be found in this hourglass model. That is because all the abstract is, is a summary of the article itself. You should write this section last, and it should cover all parts concisely.

For a much more detailed explanation of the structure of scientific writing, consult the E-Skills Learning Platform. Below are some additional guidelines to engage readers.

3.2 Writing an Introduction

The introduction serves three purposes:

  • Convince the reader that the article is worth reading;
  • Place the study presented into a larger context;
  • Summarize related work.

First collect relevant information: Seek references supporting your claims. Then reorganize all relevant information you found into a cohesive story. Your story should naturally build up to a question that is then addressed by your study. (Whether you include the research question literally is a matter of style.)

Ideally, people actually keep reading your article after the first sentence. To help engage the reader, consider the following examples of opening sentences:

Poor opening sentences

  1. In this article, the effectiveness of three different synthetic nitrogen fertilizers are compared.
  2. Synthetic nitrogen fertilizers are important because they increase the yield of crops.4,5
  3. Most nitrogen fertilizers are manufactured by the Haber-Bosch process.4

Let’s go over these examples: (1) is bad, because you should never start with what you did, first convince the reader that it needed be done. Unless you invented faster-than-light travel, this is not grabbing anyone’s attention. (2) is not good either, because simply stating that something is important is hardly convincing; it undersells how important fertilizers are. (3) should probably be placed somewhere in the introduction, but makes for a poor opening sentence. Unless the paper is specifically about the process, why should anyone care about it at this point?

Good opening sentences

  1. Synthetic nitrogen fertilizers have revolutionized modern agriculture.
  2. Virtually all modern agriculture uses synthetic nitrogen fertilizers.
  3. Up to half of all crop yield in the world can be attributed to synthetic nitrogen fertilizers.4,5

Instead, as shown here, start with an interesting related fact, like (4), or convey the magnitude of the problem you are studying like in (5) or (6). If you make a claim that is not widely accepted within your field of research, include a reference.

Personally, I recommend that you do not try to write the opening sentence first—you will just be staring at a blank page. First organize relevant information and related work, and then write an opening sentence that catches the attention.

3.3 Sentence Readability

It is easy to wave off any difficult sentence as being simply too long. However, as the most highly cited article on scientific writing points out, sentence length itself is not what makes a sentence hard to read.6 To quote the authors:

When is a sentence too long? The creators of readability formulas would have us believe there exists some fixed number of words (the favorite is 29) past which a sentence is too hard to read. We disagree. We have seen 10-word sentences that are virtually impenetrable and […] 100-word sentences that flow effortlessly to their points of resolution.

The take-away point here is to optimize readability, not sentence length.

The language of science is English, and sentences in the English language have one or more stress positions. Use this position to place the most important piece of information a sentence carries, and your sentences will be easier to read.

If English is not your strongest skill, you have plenty of time to develop it during your bachelor. Read more, write more, consider following an online course, and ask others to proofread your reports.

3.4 Referencing

Whenever you want to point a reader to relevant scientific literature, you use a citation. You can find several examples throughout this page. It is uncommon to literally quote findings from other articles. Instead, most of the time you should paraphrase.

A reference serves one of two functions:

  • To support a claim you make, by citing evidence from scientific studies;
  • To give proper attribution to the work of others (because you used their original ideas, their developed methodology, their software implementation, etc.).

There are several great search engines for finding articles to read or reference. I recommend using PubMed, or Web of Science (accessed through Leiden University).

You should not use references as:

  • An appeal to authority. Just because it is published does not make it true—always judge the quality of the article you cite;
  • Support for a well known fact, or generally accepted theory within your field of research. This is superfluous;
  • Support for a claim, when the majority of articles actually refute this claim (cherry-picking).
