Chapter 3 Scientific Writing

Skills developed in this chapter: Personal development

3.1 Overall Structure

Whether you’re writing a simple report for a course, an original research article, a review, or even a meta-analysis, any scientific article should contain at least the following:

  • What did you study? (Introduction)
  • Why did you study it? (Introduction)
  • How did you study it? (Methods)
  • What did you observe? (Results)
  • What do you conclude? (Discussion)

Hourglass model

A simple guideline is to start broad, explain the context, get progressively more specific, and finally end broad again, explaining the biological relevance of your findings (fig. 3.1). Some journals require the methods section to be placed at the end of the article but the style in which each section should be written is the same as shown here.

Figure 3.1: The hourglass model of scientific writing. The beginning and end of an article provide context, while the middle explains the specifics of the particular study.

The methods and results sections are the easiest to write. You literally just write down what you have done, and what you have observed as a result of doing so. The aim here is to be clear and objective. The only difficulty is getting used to the style of language in scientific articles, but after reading just several scientific articles within the field of research you are writing about, you should already get the hang of it.

The hard parts are the introduction and the discussion. In both of these sections, The discussion goes beyond the results, explaining not only how to interpret them, but also what relevance they have for the field as a whole.

You may have noticed that the abstract is nowhere to be found in this hourglass model. That is because all the abstract is, is a summary of the article itself. You should write this section last, and it should cover all parts concisely.

The conclusion is also not mentioned in figure 3.1. This is because it is simply part of the discussion, along with the limitations and angles for future research.

Introduction

The introduction serves three purposes:

  • Convince the reader that the article is worth reading;
  • Place the study presented into a larger context;
  • Summarize related work.

First collect relevant information: Seek references supporting your claims. Then reorganize all relevant information you found into a cohesive story. Your story should naturally build up to a question that is then addressed by your study.

Poor build-up of a story

Fertilizer is important for crop yield […] Some fertilizers are better than others […] We compared three fertilizers in terms of increased yield.

Good build-up of a story

Ideally, people actually keep reading your article after the first sentence.

Poor opening sentences

  1. In this article, the effectiveness of three different synthetic nitrogen fertilizers are compared.
  2. Synthetic nitrogen fertilizers are important because they increase the yield of crops.4,5
  3. Synthetic nitrogen fertilizers are very important because they greatly increase the yield of crops.4,5

Good opening sentences

  1. Virtually all modern agriculture uses synthetic nitrogen fertilizers.
  2. Up to half of all crop yield in the world can be attributed to synthetic nitrogen fertilizers.4,5
  3. The use synthetic nitrogen fertilizers has revolutionized modern agriculture.

Let’s go over these examples: (1) is bad, because you should never start with what you did, first convince the reader that it needed be done. Unless you invented faster-than-light travel, this is not grabbing anyone’s attention. (2) is not good either, because it undersells how important fertilizers are. (3) is still not good, because it illustrates what is wrong with (2): Simply saying something is important

Personally, I recommend that you do not try to write the opening sentence first—you will just be staring at a blank page. First organize relevant information and related work, and then write an opening sentence that catches the attention.

3.3 Sentence Structure

It is easy to wave off any difficult sentence as being simply too long. However, as the most highly cited article on scientific writing points out, sentence length itself is not what makes a sentence hard to read.6 To quote the authors:

When is a sentence too long? The creators of readability formulas would have us believe there exists some fixed number of words (the favorite is 29) past which a sentence is too hard to read. We disagree. We have seen 10-word sentences that are virtually impenetrable and […] 100-word sentences that flow effortlessly to their points of resolution.

The take-away point here is to optimize readability, not sentence length.
